APPENDIX E: LOGIC MODEL

UAPB Robotics Futures: The UAPB Alumni Mentorship Network for AI & Robotics Pathways

INPUTS
  • Financial: $2M FIPSE grant over 48 months
  • Personnel: Project Director (0.25 FTE), Program Coordinator (1.0 FTE), Faculty Lead (release time), CS faculty, external evaluator
  • Partners: St. Louis Public Schools, Arkansas LEAs, UAPB Alumni Association, FIRST Mid-America
  • Facilities: UAPB CS labs, residence halls, conference rooms
  • Technology: High-performance computing, robotics equipment (roboRIO, Limelight cameras, Jetson Nano), laptops
  • Expertise: UAPB CS faculty AI/ML knowledge, alumni technical skills, K-12 teaching experience
  • Curriculum: AI & Pedagogy Micro-credential, Robotics AI Toolkit, Bridge Institute coursework
  • UAPB Infrastructure: Office of Sponsored Programs, Housing & Dining, IT Services, Admissions
▼
ACTIVITIES

Activity 1: Alumni AI Mentor Corps

  • Recruit 80 UAPB alumni (20/year) from St. Louis and Arkansas
  • Deliver 20-hour AI & Pedagogy Micro-credential training
  • Certify mentors in AI literacy, computational thinking pedagogy, robotics systems, cultural responsiveness
  • Conduct background checks and orientation

Activity 2: AI-Infused Robotics Support

  • Match mentors with 12 FIRST Robotics teams (6 SLPS, 6 Arkansas)
  • Conduct weekly virtual code reviews (Sept-April)
  • Provide monthly on-site Build Day workshops (Jan-March)
  • Implement mandatory AI Challenge for all teams

Activity 3: Teacher Professional Development

  • Host 3-day summer PD institutes for robotics coaches
  • Distribute Robotics AI Toolkit with 20 lesson plans
  • Facilitate year-round professional learning community
  • Align curriculum with MO and AR CS standards

Activity 4: UAPB AI & Robotics Bridge Institute

  • Select 30 rising juniors/seniors annually from supported teams
  • Deliver 4-week residential summer program at UAPB
  • Teach advanced AI, computer vision, robotics
  • Provide college preparation and FAFSA support
  • Host industry panels and UAPB faculty consultations
▼
OUTPUTS
  • 80 alumni mentors trained and certified in AI pedagogy
  • 12 FIRST Robotics teams supported annually (48 team-years total)
  • 400+ K-12 students engaged in AI-enhanced robotics (cumulative)
  • 40+ robotics coaches/teachers completing summer PD
  • Robotics AI Toolkit distributed to 40+ teachers
  • 120 high school students completing Bridge Institute (30/year x 4 years)
  • 480+ weekly virtual code review sessions conducted
  • 144 on-site Build Day workshops delivered
  • 12 teams completing AI Integration Challenge annually
  • HBCU Robotics Mentorship Playbook published and disseminated
▼

▼
IMPACT
  • Educational Equity: Underrepresented students gain access to high-quality AI education and culturally responsive STEM mentorship, reducing disparities in AI literacy and CS preparation.
  • Workforce Diversity: Increased representation of Black professionals in AI and computer science fields, contributing to more equitable and less biased AI systems.
  • HBCU Strengthening: Proven model for HBCUs to leverage alumni networks for K-12 engagement, recruitment, and community impact.
  • Regional Economic Development: Expanded STEM talent pipeline supports growth of technology sectors in St. Louis, Arkansas, and surrounding regions.
  • Systemic Change: Sustainable partnerships between HBCUs, K-12 districts, and industry create lasting infrastructure for broadening participation in computing.

ASSUMPTIONS

  • UAPB alumni in St. Louis and Arkansas are willing and able to volunteer as mentors.
  • Partner school districts will provide access to robotics teams and support mentor engagement.
  • Teachers will participate in professional development and implement AI curriculum.
  • Students will engage authentically with mentors and AI learning activities.
  • FIRST Robotics provides a motivating context for advanced CS learning.
  • Micro-credential training adequately prepares mentors for effective teaching.
  • Bridge Institute participants will apply to and enroll in postsecondary STEM programs.

EXTERNAL FACTORS

  • Economic conditions: Recession or job market changes may affect mentor availability or student college affordability.
  • School district priorities: Administrative turnover or policy changes could affect robotics program continuity.
  • COVID-19 or public health events: May necessitate virtual delivery or program modifications.
  • Technology changes: Rapid evolution of AI tools and platforms requires curriculum agility.
  • Competition landscape: Changes to FIRST Robotics game design may affect relevance of AI components.
  • Funding landscape: Availability of district, state, or philanthropic funds for sustainability planning.
