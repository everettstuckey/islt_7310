ABSTRACT

UAPB Robotics Futures addresses Absolute Priority 2 by establishing a cross-state mentorship pipeline connecting University of Arkansas at Pine Bluff alumni with FIRST Robotics teams in St. Louis Public Schools (SLPS) and targeted Arkansas districts. The project targets the "AI Divide" by mobilizing HBCU alumniâ€”professionals in engineering, CS, and educationâ€”to serve as technical mentors. Key activities include: (1) Developing an "AI Mentor Micro-credential" to train alumni in pedagogy and AI literacy; (2) Embedding AI/Computer Vision modules into the standard FIRST Robotics curriculum; (3) Providing professional development for K-12 teachers; and (4) Hosting a 4-week "UAPB AI & Robotics Bridge Institute" for high school participants. Over 48 months, the project will support 12 robotics teams annually, serving 400+ students, training 80 alumni mentors, and creating a replicable model for HBCU-led K-12 STEAM engagement.

PROJECT NARRATIVE

A. SIGNIFICANCE (30 Points)

Addressing the AI Opportunity Gap

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is reshaping the workforce, yet access to high-quality AI and Computer Science (CS) instruction remains inequitable. St. Louis Public Schools (SLPS) and rural Arkansas districts serve student populations that are historically underrepresented in STEM. While many of these schools attempt to field FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC) or FIRST Tech Challenge (FTC) teams, they often lack the technical mentorship required to move beyond basic mechanics into advanced computing, specifically AI, machine learning, and autonomous programming.

The UAPB Solution: Culturally Responsive Mentorship

This project is significant because it activates an untapped resource: the UAPB Alumni Network. St. Louis is a major hub for UAPB graduates. By training these alumniâ€”who share lived experiences with the studentsâ€”to serve as "AI Robotics Mentors," we solve two problems:

  1. The Technical Gap: Alumni provide the expertise needed to teach students how to program robots using AI (e.g., computer vision for object detection).
  2. The Representation Gap: Students see successful Black engineers and technologists, directly influencing their postsecondary aspirations.

Magnitude of Results

This project moves beyond "exposure." By integrating AI literacy into the competitive ecosystem of FIRST Robotics, students apply AI concepts to solve tangible problems. This creates a direct pipeline from 8th grade through high school graduation, leading to increased enrollment in CS/AI programs at UAPB and peer institutions.

B. QUALITY OF THE PROJECT DESIGN (45 Points)

The project is designed around four distinct activity pillars:

Activity 1: The Alumni AI Mentor Corps (Train the Trainer)

UAPB will recruit cohorts of 20 alumni annually (80 total). Before entering schools, mentors will complete a 20-hour "AI & Pedagogy Micro-credential" developed by UAPB Computer Science faculty.

  • Curriculum: How to teach AI ethics, explaining neural networks to 14-year-olds, and fostering "Computational Thinking."
  • Outcome: A certified workforce of volunteer mentors capable of translating high-level tech to K-12 students.

Activity 2: AI-Infused Robotics Support (School Year)

Mentors are matched with robotics teams in SLPS and Arkansas. Support is hybrid:

  • Weekly Virtual Code Reviews: Mentors review student code (Java/Python) via Zoom, specifically focusing on autonomous modes and sensor integration.
  • On-Site Build Days: Mentors travel to schools monthly during "Build Season" (Jan-March) to assist with complex systems.
  • The AI Challenge: Teams will be tasked with integrating a specific AI component (e.g., using a Limelight camera for vision tracking) regardless of competition requirements, ensuring AI learning occurs.

Activity 3: Teacher Professional Development

Consistent with Priority 2(f), UAPB will partner with SLPS and Arkansas LEAs to provide summer PD workshops for the teachers/coaches leading these teams.

  • Focus: "From Blocks to Python: Integrating AI in the Classroom."
  • Resource: Teachers receive a "Robotics AI Toolkit" with lesson plans mapped to state CS standards.

Activity 4: The UAPB AI & Robotics Bridge Institute (Summer)

A 4-week residential summer program at the UAPB campus for rising high school juniors/seniors selected from the supported teams.

  • Content: Advanced AI coursework, college prep, and a "Robot-in-3-Days" style design challenge.
  • Goal: Solidify the pathway from high school robotics to UAPB matriculation.

UAPB will contract an independent external evaluator to conduct a mixed-methods evaluation.

  3. Postsecondary Intent: 75% of graduating seniors in the program will apply to a STEM or AI-related postsecondary program.
  4. Expansion: Successfully support 12 teams annually by Year 2.

INSTRUMENT 1: AI LITERACY SCALE

Source and Validation

Long, D., & Magerko, B. (2020). "What is AI Literacy? Competencies and Design Considerations." Proceedings of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems, 1-16. DOI: 10.1145/3313831.3376727

This validated instrument measures five core competencies of AI literacy: (1) What is AI?, (2) What Can AI Do?, (3) How Does AI Work?, (4) How Should AI Be Used?, and (5) How Do People Perceive AI? The scale has demonstrated strong reliability (Cronbach's α = 0.89) across diverse populations.

Administration Protocol

Pre-Test: Administered at the beginning of the robotics season (September) to all participating students
Post-Test: Administered at the conclusion of the robotics season (April) using identical items
Duration: 20-25 minutes
Format: Online survey via Qualtrics

Sample Items

Domain 1: What is AI?

Rate your agreement with the following statements on a scale from 1 (Strongly Disagree) to 5 (Strongly Agree):

□ I can explain what artificial intelligence means to someone who doesn't know.
□ I can give examples of AI technologies that I use in my everyday life.
□ I understand the difference between AI and regular computer programs.

Domain 2: What Can AI Do?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I can describe at least three tasks that AI systems can perform.
□ I understand the limitations of what current AI can and cannot do.
□ I can identify situations where AI would be more effective than humans.

Domain 3: How Does AI Work?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I understand how AI systems learn from data.
□ I can explain what a "neural network" is in simple terms.
□ I understand how training data affects AI system performance.

Domain 4: How Should AI Be Used?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I can identify ethical issues that might arise from using AI.
□ I understand what "algorithmic bias" means.
□ I can explain why fairness is important in AI design.

Domain 5: How Do People Perceive AI?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I understand why some people are excited about AI while others are concerned.
□ I can explain common misconceptions about AI.
□ I have thought about how AI might affect my future career.

Note: The full validated instrument contains 25 items across the five domains. Scoring is calculated as the mean response across items within each domain, with an overall AI Literacy score computed as the average of the five domain scores. A 0.5 standard deviation increase (approximately 0.5 points on the 5-point scale) represents educationally significant growth.

APPENDIX F: DATA COLLECTION AND MANAGEMENT PLAN

1. OVERVIEW AND PURPOSE

This Data Collection and Management Plan describes the procedures, protocols, and safeguards that will govern all data collection, storage, analysis, and reporting activities for the UAPB Robotics Futures project. The plan ensures compliance with the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), institutional Institutional Review Board (IRB) requirements, and the ethical principles articulated in the Belmont Report.

Key Principles:

  • Protection of Human Subjects: All data collection involving minors will be reviewed and approved by the UAPB Institutional Review Board prior to implementation.
  • Informed Consent/Assent: Parent/guardian consent and student assent will be obtained for all participants under age 18.
  • Confidentiality: Personally identifiable information (PII) will be protected through de-identification, secure storage, and restricted access protocols.
  • Transparency: Participants will be informed about data use, risks, and their right to withdraw.
  • Data Quality: Systematic procedures will ensure data accuracy, completeness, and reliability.

3. INFORMED CONSENT AND ASSENT PROCEDURES

3.1 IRB Approval

Prior to any data collection, the project will submit a comprehensive IRB application to the University of Arkansas at Pine Bluff Institutional Review Board. The application will describe all data collection instruments, procedures, risks, benefits, and safeguards. Data collection will not begin until IRB approval is obtained.

3.2 Parent/Guardian Consent (for students under 18)

At the beginning of each robotics season, parents/guardians of all participating students will receive a detailed consent form explaining:

  • Purpose of the research/evaluation
  • Types of data to be collected (surveys, academic records, observations)
  • How data will be used (program evaluation, annual reports to funder, research publications)
  • Risks and benefits of participation
  • Procedures to protect confidentiality
  • Right to withdraw at any time without penalty
  • Contact information for project staff and UAPB IRB

Consent forms will be available in English and Spanish. Parents may consent for their child to participate in the robotics program while declining participation in research/evaluation activities; student participation in robotics is not contingent on research consent.

3.3 Student Assent (for students under 18)

In addition to parent consent, students will provide written assent using age-appropriate language. The assent form will explain the study in simple terms and emphasize that participation is voluntary and can be discontinued at any time.

3.4 Adult Consent (for alumni mentors and teachers)

Adult participants (mentors and teachers) will provide informed consent before participating in surveys, focus groups, or interviews. Consent forms will explain data use, confidentiality measures, and the right to withdraw.

4. DATA SECURITY AND CONFIDENTIALITY

4.1 De-Identification

All student data will be de-identified before analysis and reporting. Each student will be assigned a unique research ID number. A master key linking names to ID numbers will be stored separately from the data in a password-protected file accessible only to the Program Coordinator and External Evaluator.

4.2 Data Storage

Electronic Data:

  • All electronic data will be stored on UAPB's secure servers with encrypted backup.
  • Survey data collected via Qualtrics will be stored on UAPB's institutional Qualtrics account with SSL encryption and two-factor authentication enabled.
  • Access to data files will be restricted to authorized project personnel through role-based permissions.
  • All computers and devices used to access data will be password-protected and encrypted.

Paper Data:

  • Paper consent forms and any paper surveys will be stored in locked filing cabinets in the Program Coordinator's office.
  • Paper records will be scanned and uploaded to secure servers, then retained in locked storage for the duration of the project.

4.3 Data Transmission

When data must be transmitted between project staff and the External Evaluator:

  • Files will be encrypted using AES-256 encryption
  • Secure file transfer protocols (SFTP or encrypted email via Virtru) will be used
  • No PII will be transmitted via unencrypted email or cloud storage services

4.4 FERPA Compliance

When requesting student academic records (GPA, course enrollment, standardized test scores) from partner school districts:

  • UAPB will execute FERPA-compliant data sharing agreements with each district
  • Data will be requested using student research IDs, not names, where possible
  • Districts will transmit data via secure methods (encrypted files, secure FTP)
  • UAPB will maintain data as "education records" and apply FERPA protections
  • No student-level data will be publicly reported or shared with third parties without explicit consent

4.5 Data Retention and Disposal

Data will be retained for three years following the submission of the final grant report to the Department of Education, consistent with federal record retention requirements. After this period:

  • Electronic data files will be securely deleted (overwritten, not merely deleted)
  • Paper records will be shredded using a cross-cut shredder
  • The master key linking names to research IDs will be destroyed first, rendering remaining data permanently de-identified

5. DATA QUALITY ASSURANCE

5.1 Survey Administration Protocols

  • Standardized Instructions: All surveys will include clear, standardized instructions to ensure consistent administration.
  • Pilot Testing: Survey instruments will be pilot tested with small groups prior to full implementation to identify ambiguous items or technical issues.
  • Response Validation: Qualtrics will be configured with response validation rules (e.g., required fields, range checks) to minimize missing or invalid data.
  • Completion Monitoring: Program Coordinator will monitor survey completion rates and send reminders to non-respondents to maximize response rates.

5.2 Data Entry and Verification

  • Paper surveys (if any) will be double-entered by two different staff members to identify transcription errors.
  • Discrepancies will be resolved by referring to the original paper form.
  • Data files will be checked for out-of-range values, duplicate records, and logical inconsistencies.

5.3 Documentation

The External Evaluator will maintain comprehensive documentation including:

  • Data collection protocols and field notes
  • Codebooks defining all variables, response options, and coding schemes
  • Data cleaning logs documenting all corrections and transformations
  • Analytic syntax/code used for statistical analyses (to ensure reproducibility)

6. DATA ANALYSIS AND REPORTING

6.1 Quantitative Analysis

The External Evaluator will conduct quantitative analyses using SPSS or R statistical software. Key analyses include:

  • Descriptive Statistics: Frequencies, means, standard deviations for all outcome measures
  • Pre-Post Comparisons: Paired t-tests or repeated measures ANOVA to assess change in AI Literacy, mentor self-efficacy, and student postsecondary intent
  • Effect Sizes: Cohen's d to quantify magnitude of change
  • Subgroup Analyses: Disaggregation by gender, grade level, prior CS experience to identify differential impacts
  • Outcome Tracking: Calculation of percentages meeting target benchmarks (e.g., % of students achieving 0.5 SD growth; % of seniors applying to STEM programs)

6.2 Qualitative Analysis

Focus group transcripts and open-ended survey responses will be analyzed using thematic coding:

  • Transcripts will be coded independently by two researchers using NVivo software
  • Initial codes will be developed deductively (based on research questions) and inductively (emerging from data)
  • Inter-rater reliability will be assessed; disagreements will be resolved through discussion
  • Themes will be organized into meaningful categories and illustrated with representative quotes

6.3 Reporting and Dissemination

Annual Reports to Funder: UAPB will submit annual performance reports to the Department of Education including aggregate data on outputs (students served, mentors trained) and outcomes (test scores, enrollment rates). No individual-level data will be reported.

Public Dissemination: Project findings may be presented at conferences or published in peer-reviewed journals. All public dissemination will use only de-identified, aggregate data. Specific safeguards include:

  • Cell sizes < 5 will be suppressed to prevent re-identification
  • No photographs or videos of minors will be used without explicit parental consent
  • All quotes will be anonymized (names, schools, identifying details removed)

Participant Access: At the conclusion of the project, participating students, mentors, and teachers will receive a summary report of findings in accessible language. Individual students/mentors will not receive their own data.

9. CONTINGENCY PLANNING

Data Breach Response

In the unlikely event of a data breach or unauthorized access to student data:

  1. The breach will be reported immediately to the Project Director, UAPB IRB, and UAPB General Counsel.
  2. UAPB will conduct an investigation to determine the scope of the breach and individuals affected.
  3. Affected individuals (students, parents) will be notified within 72 hours consistent with applicable data breach notification laws.
  4. UAPB will offer credit monitoring or other appropriate remedies to affected individuals.
  5. The project will implement corrective actions to prevent recurrence (e.g., additional staff training, enhanced technical safeguards).
  6. The breach and response will be documented in project records and reported to the Department of Education if required.

Loss of Key Personnel

If the Program Coordinator or External Evaluator leaves the project:

  • Data files will remain secure on UAPB servers and accessible to the Project Director.
  • A replacement will be hired and provided with access credentials.
  • The replacement will complete all required training before accessing data.
  • Departing personnel will return all data files, delete local copies, and sign an acknowledgment that they will maintain confidentiality.

10. ETHICS COMMITMENTS

Beyond regulatory compliance, the UAPB Robotics Futures project commits to the following ethical principles:

  • Respect for Persons: We will honor students' autonomy, obtain meaningful consent, and provide additional protections for minors.
  • Beneficence: We will maximize benefits (high-quality mentorship, AI education) and minimize risks (no collection of sensitive data beyond what is necessary).
  • Justice: We will ensure that the benefits of research (improved AI literacy, college access) are equitably distributed and do not disproportionately burden any group.
  • Fidelity and Responsibility: We will be transparent with participants about data use, maintain their trust, and act with integrity.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: We will recognize that our participants are primarily Black students and alumni. Data collection and reporting will avoid deficit framing and instead highlight strengths, assets, and systemic barriers.

This Data Collection and Management Plan will be reviewed annually and updated as needed to reflect changes in regulations, technology, or project activities.
