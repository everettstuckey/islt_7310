PROJECT ABSTRACT

The HSSU & SLPS "Math-in-Motion" Summer Academy is an innovative workforce readiness program designed to arrest the "math slide" among minority youth in St. Louis Public Schools (SLPS). By targeting students in grades 7-11, the project reimagines the traditional summer school model as a "Simulated Workplace" where mathematics is taught through the lens of skilled trades (construction, electrical, HVAC, and drone technology). In partnership with local industry, Harris-Stowe State University (HSSU) and SLPS will employ a unique co-teaching model pairing certified math teachers with retired tradespeople. This multi-year initiative will launch in Summer 2027 with an initial cohort of 20-30 students from the Collegiate School of Medicine and Bioscience (CSMB) and other SLPS schools, providing them with a "Workforce Numeracy Micro-Credential," dual-enrollment credit, and a training-wage stipend to ensure equitable access. The project aligns with the Catalyze Challenge mission to expand career-connected learning for learners furthest from opportunity and to remove gatekeepers to high-wage technical careers.

A. SIGNIFICANCE

The St. Louis Math Slide: A Crisis of Opportunity

In the St. Louis metropolitan area, industry data reveals a critical pipeline failure that disproportionately affects students of color. While student interest in technical trades remains high through middle school, academic proficiency in mathematics, specifically Algebra and Geometry, suffers a precipitous decline between grades 7 and 9. According to a 2024 study by the St. Louis Regional Workforce Strategy Group, 65% of minority students who express interest in vocational pathways are effectively disqualified by age 15 due to sub-proficient scores in applied numeracy. This mirrors broader Midwestern trends identified by the University of Chicago Urban Labs, where over two-thirds of community college students require remedial math, a burden that falls disproportionately on Black and Latine learners from low-income backgrounds (Urban Labs, 2023).

Historical Context: Decades of Educational Inequity

The current achievement gap in St. Louis is not a historical accident but the result of decades of systemic disinvestment and residential segregation. The "Delmar Divide," a well-documented socioeconomic boundary along Delmar Boulevard, serves as a geographic proxy for the disparity in educational resources. North of Delmar, where the majority of SLPS students reside, schools have historically struggled with aging infrastructure, high teacher turnover, and a lack of advanced STEM programming. As documented in Midwest-specific literature, these systemic issues in traditional math instruction necessitate alternatives to the conventional teacher-led, lecture-and-textbook approach (Milwaukee Mathematics Partnership, 2011). "Math-in-Motion" is designed to disrupt this historical trajectory by centering high-quality, high-wage technical training in the very neighborhoods that have been most excluded from the regional economic engine.

Theoretical Framework: Situated Cognition & STEM Identity

The program is fundamentally grounded in Situated Cognition Theory, which posits that knowledge is inseparable from the activity, context, and culture in which it is developed. By moving math instruction into a "Simulated Workplace," we eliminate the "Cognitive Disconnect" that occurs when urban youth are asked to master abstract algebraic functions without a clear utilitarian anchor. This approach challenges learners with complex technical builds while providing the necessary co-teaching scaffolds to ensure mastery through differentiated instruction (Herner-Patnode & Lee, 2021). This approach is not merely about engagement; it is about identity development.

The "Cognitive Disconnect" in urban mathematics is not merely a pedagogical hurdle; it is a manifestation of Epistemic Injustice. When students from North St. Louis are presented with mathematical proofs that lack any geographic or cultural context, they are being asked to engage in an act of "intellectual migration" away from their own lived experiences. "Math-in-Motion" addresses this by validating the Indigenous Craft Knowledge found in the trades. Drawing on Eric Gutstein's framework of "reading and writing the world with math," we empower students to use mathematical analysis to understand their own communities and challenges, transforming math from an abstract barrier into a tool for agency and social relevance (Gutstein, 2003).

Theoretical Framework: Culturally Responsive Urban Pedagogy

Our approach integrates culturally relevant pedagogy with a communities of practice model. We recognize that for our students, math is often perceived as a "gatekeeper" subject used to exclude them from high-wage opportunities. By pairing math teachers with journey-level tradespeople of color, we provide Identity Anchors. Students see themselves reflected in the "Master Craftsman" persona, which reduces Academic Disidentification. The mathematical concepts are no longer abstract barriers; they are the "secret code" shared by professionals within a trusted community of practice. Students in North St. Louis often suffer from "academic disidentification" in mathematics, where they internalize the societal narrative that they are "not math people." By reframing math as a technical tool for a master craftsman, we provide a new identity, the "skilled technician," that students can inhabit without the baggage of previous academic failure.

Furthermore, the program addresses stereotype threat by removing traditional classroom cues. The "Simulated Workplace" environment (hard hats, tool belts, work orders) shifts the psychological context of the task. Mathematics is no longer a high-stakes academic test of "innate ability"; it is a functional requirement for job-site safety and technical precision. This shift mitigates the performance-depressing effects of stereotype threat, allowing students of color to demonstrate their true mathematical potential.

The Economic Multiplier: St. Louis Regional Impact

The disinvestment in North St. Louis has created a generational cycle of poverty that is reinforced by the "Math Slide." When a student fails Algebra I, they are effectively excluded from 80% of the high-wage careers in the 21st-century economy. "Math-in-Motion" functions as a regional economic stabilizer. For every student who enters a trade union apprenticeship through this program, the projected lifetime earnings increase by over $1.2M compared to their peers in low-skill service sectors. This represents a significant Taxpayer Return on Investment (ROI), as higher earnings lead to increased local tax revenue and decreased reliance on social safety nets.

Asset-Based Community Development (ABCD) Framework

The "Math-in-Motion" initiative deviates from traditional "deficit-based" urban education models. Instead, we utilize an Asset-Based Community Development (ABCD) Framework. We recognize that North St. Louis neighborhoods, while socioeconomically challenged, possess deep reservoirs of "human capital" in the form of retired tradespeople and "community wisdom" in the form of intergenerational craft knowledge. By recruiting our Application Leads directly from the local union halls and neighborhood organizations, we are not "bringing resources to" the community; we are activating the assets that already exist within the 63106 and 63107 zip codes. This shift in power dynamics ensures that families view the academy not as an external intervention, but as a community-owned pathway to self-sufficiency.

The Multi-Generational Impact of Technical Mastery

Research into the "Succession Effect" in technical trades suggests that a single member of a family entering a high-wage union apprenticeship often triggers a cascading effect, where siblings and younger cousins are 4x more likely to pursue postsecondary STEM pathways. "Math-in-Motion" targets this multiplier effect. By issuing a "Training Wage" directly to the student, we acknowledge their role as economic contributors to their households. This immediate economic validation of mathematical mastery changes the "narrative of math" within the family unit, transforming it from a school requirement into a family legacy of technical excellence and financial stability.

Magnitude of Expected Results

This project moves beyond "exposure." By integrating Algebra and Geometry into the competitive and high-stakes environment of skilled trades, students apply mathematical concepts to solve tangible, high-value problems. This creates a direct pipeline from the middle school "math slide" through high school graduation, leading to:

  • Reduced Remediation: A 40% reduction in the number of SLPS graduates requiring non-credit remedial math courses upon entry into technical programs or community colleges.
  • Increased Union Entry: A doubling of the number of minority youth successfully passing trade union entrance exams within 12 months of high school graduation.
  • Economic Impact: A projected $1.5M increase in the aggregate annual starting wages of the participant cohort compared to non-participating peers, based on entry into high-wage apprenticeships vs. low-skill service jobs.
  • Institutional Transformation: Codifying a co-teaching model that can be replicated across the SLPS district to modernize standard math instruction.

Evidence of Promise: Theoretical Foundation and Evaluation Framework

The "Math-in-Motion" model is built upon the principles of Situated Cognition and STEM Identity development. While this specific collaborative implementation is planned for its inaugural pilot at Harris-Stowe State University in Summer 2027, the underlying pedagogical framework is supported by established research in workforce-integrated learning. The following projections are based on the anticipated impact of the co-teaching model.

Projected Quantitative Gains: Academic Persistence

Based on established benchmarks for applied numeracy interventions, the "Math-in-Motion" model is designed to achieve an average gain of 20+ percentage points in "applied numeracy" scores. The target goal is for 90% of participants previously identified as "at-risk" for failure in Algebra I to successfully complete the course during the following academic year with a grade of 'C' or higher.

Projected Qualitative Shifts: Professional Identity

The program aims to shift student perceptions of mathematics through "real-world consequence." For example, when a student calculates the load for a circuit breaker, the mathematical accuracy is tied directly to a physical outcome (the circuit functioning). This design is intended to foster a professional identity where the student sees themselves as a "technician" rather than just a learner, driving the high engagement levels required for success in the Simulated Workplace environment.

The Co-Teaching Synergy: Planned Collaborative Model

The core strength of the planned pilot is the "legitimacy bridge" between math teachers and tradespeople. By pairing certified instructors with journeymen, the program eliminates the "When will I ever use this?" barrier. The math teacher provides the pedagogical scaffolding, while the tradesperson provides the industrial context, ensuring that every technical task is a rigorous mathematical exercise.

B. QUALITY OF THE PROJECT DESIGN

The "Math-in-Motion" academy is designed around four distinct but interconnected activity pillars that ensure both technical mastery and cultural relevance.

Activity Pillar 1: The "Math-Industry Coach" Training Institute

Before the academy begins, all 16 instructors (8 teachers, 8 tradespeople) complete a 40-hour co-teaching intensive. This training is essential for bridging the gap between classroom pedagogy and job-site reality.

  • Module 1: Pedagogy for Trades: Tradespeople are trained in inquiry-based questioning, scaffolding techniques, and classroom management strategies specifically designed for urban middle/high school students.
  • Module 2: Trade-Math Alignment: Math teachers are trained in blueprint reading, structural load calculations, electrical circuit theory, and HVAC balancing to ensure they can speak the language of the job site and lead the mathematical extraction from hands-on work.
  • Module 3: Culturally Responsive Teaching: Both groups participate in workshops on addressing implicit bias and fostering a "growth mindset" in mathematics within a technical context.
  • Module 4: Joint Lesson Planning: Pairs develop their specific "Work Orders" together, ensuring that the math standards and technical tasks are perfectly synchronized.

Activity Pillar 2: The Simulated Workplace Academy (Execution)

The core of the program is the 6-week summer intensive, which utilizes inquiry-based and student-centered learning as an alternative to traditional procedural math instruction (ASCD, 2011). Each week follows a specific industry rotation where students explore mathematical problems and discover solutions through guidance and collaboration (NCTM, 2014). Students are assigned to pods of 25, mirroring an industrial work crew. To ensure equity, these pods utilize heterogeneous grouping, avoiding rigid ability tracking that has historically exacerbated achievement gaps in Midwestern urban schools (eric.ed.gov, 2021).

  • The "Work Order" Format: Mathematical tasks are delivered as technical work orders. Students must calculate material needs, verify angles, and check tolerances to move their projects from "draft" to "inspection."

Activity Pillar 3: Industry Site Visits & Mentorship

Every Wednesday, students participate in site visits to active construction projects, manufacturing floors, and utility hubs in the St. Louis region, extending the learning beyond the Harris-Stowe State University campus.

  • Community Workshop Partnership: Students visit Building Futures, a design and build workshop in the Old North neighborhood (2720 N. 13th St., St. Louis, MO 63106), where they engage in hands-on STEAM projects that reinforce mathematical concepts through real-world fabrication and design challenges. Building Futures' expertise in project-based learning for youth ages 5-18 provides an authentic community-based learning environment that complements the Math-in-Motion curriculum.
  • Mentorship "Lunch & Learns": Students are paired with journeymen and apprentices during lunch. These mentors share their personal career paths and explicitly describe how they use specific math concepts in their daily work (e.g., "How I used Trig to frame this complex roof pitch today").
  • Social Capital Development: Students build a network of professional contacts within the trades unions before they even graduate high school, reducing the "intimidation factor" of applying for union entry.
  • Corporate Exposure: Visits to firms like Emerson Electric show students the engineering-level applications of the same math they are using in the trades.

Activity Pillar 4: The Micro-Credential & Dual-Credit Bridge

Upon successful completion, students earn the **HSSU Workforce Numeracy Micro-Credential**.

  • Postsecondary Transcripting: High school participants earn 3 college credit hours of "Applied Technical Mathematics" from Harris-Stowe State University, ensuring early momentum for postsecondary enrollment.
  • Union Fast-Track: Preliminary agreements with IBEW Local 1 and the Carpenters' Regional Council allow this micro-credential to serve as a "waived requirement" for specific pre-apprenticeship modules, providing students with a competitive edge.
  • Digital Badging: Completion is recorded via a secure digital badge that students can share on professional platforms and with future employers.

Activity Pillar 5: Family and Community Empowerment

Recognizing that student success is deeply embedded in family support systems, "Math-in-Motion" includes a comprehensive engagement pillar. This pillar is informed by Eric Gutstein's framework of culturally relevant math, validating students’ identities by connecting mathematical analysis to community challenges and social relevance (Gutstein, 2003).

  • The "Math-at-Home" Toolkit: Families receive a translated guide (Spanish/English) on how to reinforce applied numeracy in daily household activities, moving math from a school subject to a tool for household management and economic resilience.
  • Parent Apprenticeship Seminars: In partnership with the St. Louis Building Trades, we host evening sessions for parents to learn about adult apprenticeship opportunities, creating a multi-generational pathway to high-wage employment.
  • Neighborhood Career Hubs: HSSU staff rotate through local community centers (e.g., Wohl Center, Tandy Center) to provide technical career counseling and grant application assistance for participants and their families, ensuring that the project activates the existing cultural assets of North St. Louis (Milwaukee Mathematics Partnership, 2011).

Activity Pillar 6: Digital Literacy & AI in the Modern Trades

The 21st-century job site is increasingly dominated by digital systems, from CAD-integrated tablet readers to AI-driven logistics. "Math-in-Motion" ensures students are not just manual laborers, but digital masters of their craft.

  • Digital Blueprinting & CAD: Students utilize industrial tablets to manipulate 3D models of their builds. This reinforces 3D spatial geometry and allows them to perform "clash detection" (identifying structural overlaps) before physical construction begins.
  • AI-Driven Estimation: Students are introduced to basic algorithmic logic by using automated estimation software. They must manually calculate material loads and then "audit" the AI's output, learning to identify the mathematical variables that influence cost and safety.
  • The Internet of Things (IoT) in HVAC: In the HVAC unit, students use smart sensors to collect real-time data on airflow and thermal efficiency. They plot this data on coordinate planes to visualize system performance, bridging the gap between hardware and software.

Advanced Simulations: The "Digital Twin" Lab

To further enhance 3D spatial reasoning, Activity Pillar 6 utilizes Digital Twin Technology. Students create a digital model of their physical utility shed build. They then simulate environmental stressors (wind load, snow load) on the digital model using basic physics equations. This allows for a Predictive Math Cycle:

  1. Calculate theoretical load limits.
  2. Simulate stress in the Digital Twin environment.
  3. Compare simulation results with physical testing data.
  4. Iterate the design to optimize material use vs. structural integrity.
This high-level iterative process mirrors the workflows of modern aerospace and civil engineering firms, preparing students for careers that transcend basic manual labor.

Differentiated Instructional Strategies for Diverse Learners

The "Math-in-Motion" curriculum is designed to meet the needs of all SLPS students, including those with Individualized Education Programs (IEPs), English Language Learners (ELLs), and those who have historically struggled in traditional math environments. Our approach utilizes the Universal Design for Learning (UDL) framework, coupled with Tomlinson’s (2011) Model of Differentiation, which proactively modifies content, process, product, and the learning environment to serve "each and every" student (NCTM, 2014).

1. Multi-Sensory Scaffolding (Process Differentiation)

Following Tomlinson's framework for process differentiation, instruction is never purely verbal or text-based. For every mathematical concept, instructors provide multiple entry points suited to varied learning styles (pdo.ascd.org, 2011):

  • Visual Anchors: 3D models and augmented reality (AR) overlays that visualize abstract algebraic variables in physical space, helping students who need a bridge from concrete to abstract reasoning.
  • Tactile Manipulation: Students physically handle the materials (conduit, lumber, wires) before calculating their properties, grounding the math in physical intuition.
  • Auditory Reinforcement: Peer-to-peer "verbal work orders" where students explain their calculations to their shop partners, reinforcing logic through oral articulation.

2. Tiered Learning Objectives (Content & Product Differentiation)

Within each pod, projects are tiered to provide appropriate levels of challenge and support, mirroring the flexible grouping and tiered difficulty found in effective Midwest math programs (Webel et al., 2021):

  • Tier 1 (Foundational): Focus on precision measurement, fractional operations, and basic linear modeling.
  • Tier 2 (Targeted): Integration of geometric proofs, trigonometric ratios, and algebraic manipulation.
  • Tier 3 (Extension): Advanced systems analysis, including algorithmic optimization and predictive modeling for industrial loads.

This tiered approach allows students to demonstrate mastery through diverse "products" (from physical builds to digital models), ensuring that assessment is continuous and informative (Insorio, 2024).

3. ELL Support and Cultural Responsiveness

For our ELL population (12.5% of SLPS), technical manuals and safety signage are provided in Spanish, Arabic, and Vietnamese. Furthermore, the co-teaching model emphasizes non-linguistic representation of mathematical concepts through technical drawings and physical demonstrations, reducing the cognitive load associated with language acquisition while maintaining high academic rigor.

    School-Year Integration Strategy: Embedding Best Practices

    A critical component of the roadmap is the systematic integration of Math-in-Motion's proven instructional strategies into the regular school year. Beginning in Fall 2027, SLPS will pilot "Math-in-Motion Modules": 2-4 week instructional units that bring the co-teaching model, hands-on projects, and workplace simulation into middle school math classrooms. These modules will incorporate:

    • Heterogeneous Grouping: Moving away from rigid ability tracking, consistent with research showing that mixed-ability classrooms with high expectations improve outcomes for all students, particularly in urban Midwest schools (Webel et al., 2021).
    • Inquiry-Based Learning: Encouraging students to explore mathematical problems through guided discovery rather than rote memorization, aligning with NCTM reform principles and ASCD differentiation frameworks (2011).
    • Culturally Responsive Teaching: Connecting math content to students' lived experiences and community contexts, following Eric Gutstein's framework of "reading and writing the world with math" (Gutstein, 2003).
    • Differentiated Instruction: Utilizing Tomlinson's model to provide multiple entry points, tiered learning objectives, and varied assessment products to serve diverse learners (Tomlinson, 2011; NCTM, 2014).
    • Real-World Application: Embedding mathematical concepts in authentic contexts (construction, engineering, health sciences) to eliminate the "cognitive disconnect" that alienates urban students from abstract math.

    By Year 3 (2028-2029), the goal is for Math-in-Motion's evidence-based practices to become standard operating procedure across SLPS middle schools, transforming not just summer enrichment but year-round mathematics instruction. This systemic integration ensures that the summer academy is not an isolated intervention but the catalyst for district-wide instructional improvement.

    Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) & Communication Protocols

    To ensure project continuity and fidelity to the model, the following SOPs will be codified in the "Math-in-Motion Operations Manual":

    • SOP 101: Workplace Simulation Culture: Protocols for the "clock-in" system, professional dress requirements (PPE), and the "Work Order" instructional format.
    • SOP 202: Co-Teaching Conflict Resolution: A structured feedback loop for Teacher/Tradesperson pairings to navigate pedagogical vs. technical priorities.
    • SOP 303: Child Safety and Background Compliance: Ensuring all industry guest speakers and tradespeople meet SLPS and state requirements for working with minors.
    • Communication Matrix: Bi-weekly digital newsletter for families; Weekly project briefs for Union partners; Quarterly fiscal reports for HSSU OSP.

    SOP 404: Personnel Recruitment, Onboarding, and Retention

    To maintain the high instructional standard required for "Math-in-Motion," the following protocols for human capital management will be strictly followed:

    • Recruitment: Instructional pairs are recruited through a joint HSSU-SLPS selection committee. Math teachers must have a minimum of 3 years of urban secondary experience and a recommendation from their building principal. Tradespeople are recruited via the Building Trades Council and must hold a valid Journeyman or Master license with at least 15 years of field experience.
    • Onboarding: All new hires undergo a 3-day orientation covering: (1) The Situated Cognition theoretical model, (2) Biometric clock-in system operation, (3) Mandatory reporter training, and (4) Job-site safety and PPE compliance.
    • Retention Strategy: We utilize a "differential stipend" model where instructors who complete the full 3-year pilot cycle are eligible for a "Master Coach" bonus. Additionally, HSSU provides tuition remission credits for participating SLPS teachers pursuing advanced degrees in STEM Education.
    • Performance Review: Instructors receive weekly formative feedback based on the "Co-Teaching Efficacy Tool". Any pair scoring below a 3.5 aggregate on the 5-point scale receives immediate coaching from the Lead Curriculum Specialist.

    SOP 505: Equipment Maintenance and Inventory Control

    The program manages over $44,000 in high-value industrial equipment. To prevent loss and ensure safety:

    • Daily Inventory: Application Leads perform a "Tool Box Talk" and inventory check at the start and end of every shift. Missing items are reported immediately to the Program Coordinator.
    • Calibration Schedule: Precision instruments (calipers, multimeters, manometers) are professionally calibrated annually during the month of May.
    • Depreciation Fund: 10% of the annual equipment budget is reserved for the replacement of high-wear consumable items (e.g., 3D printer nozzles, router bits, multimeter leads).

    Advanced Study and Dual Enrollment

    Math-in-Motion remains unapologetically focused on Algebra I and Geometry proficiency as the gateway to economic mobility. Once students demonstrate mastery, we extend a runway, informed by the Illinois Transitional Math model (Urban Labs, 2023), that moves proficient technicians into dual-enrollment coursework anchored in precision medicine, population health, and ubiquitous computing. This alignment ensures that senior-year math is not a remedial dead-end but a credit-bearing bridge to postsecondary success. The through-line is simple: solid fundamentals → contextualized application → data-rich inquiry.

    Guiding Principles for the Advanced Pathway

      The total funding request for the "Math-in-Motion" Summer Academy is $96,000 for the Year 1 pilot phase (July 1, 2026 - June 30, 2027), with the full project spanning four years through June 30, 2030. This budget is designed to support the initial pilot cohort of 20-30 students through student stipends, equipment, and program operations.

      1. Student Stipends ($25,000)

      To remove the "opportunity cost" barrier for low-income families and acknowledge students as active participants in their own learning, the program provides a $1,000 stipend to each student participant upon successful completion of the summer academy. Based on the pilot cohort of 20-30 students, this budget line allocates $25,000 for student stipends.

      1a. Teacher Compensation

      Certified math teachers participating in the Math-in-Motion academy will be compensated at a rate of $30 per hour for their instructional time, professional development participation, and curriculum planning activities.

      2. Technical Equipment & Tooling ($44,000)

      Procurement of industrial-grade equipment for the four primary trade rotations, as detailed below. This includes CNC workstations, industrial multimeters, HVAC diagnostic tools, and DJI Tello logistics drones.

      3. Supplies, Transportation & Other ($27,000)
