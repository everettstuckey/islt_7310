APPENDIX D: EVALUATION INSTRUMENTS

INSTRUMENT 1: AI LITERACY SCALE

Source and Validation

Long, D., & Magerko, B. (2020). "What is AI Literacy? Competencies and Design Considerations." Proceedings of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems, 1-16. DOI: 10.1145/3313831.3376727

This validated instrument measures five core competencies of AI literacy: (1) What is AI?, (2) What Can AI Do?, (3) How Does AI Work?, (4) How Should AI Be Used?, and (5) How Do People Perceive AI? The scale has demonstrated strong reliability (Cronbach's α = 0.89) across diverse populations.

Administration Protocol

Pre-Test: Administered at the beginning of the robotics season (September) to all participating students
Post-Test: Administered at the conclusion of the robotics season (April) using identical items
Duration: 20-25 minutes
Format: Online survey via Qualtrics

Sample Items

Domain 1: What is AI?

Rate your agreement with the following statements on a scale from 1 (Strongly Disagree) to 5 (Strongly Agree):

□ I can explain what artificial intelligence means to someone who doesn't know.
□ I can give examples of AI technologies that I use in my everyday life.
□ I understand the difference between AI and regular computer programs.

Domain 2: What Can AI Do?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I can describe at least three tasks that AI systems can perform.
□ I understand the limitations of what current AI can and cannot do.
□ I can identify situations where AI would be more effective than humans.

Domain 3: How Does AI Work?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I understand how AI systems learn from data.
□ I can explain what a "neural network" is in simple terms.
□ I understand how training data affects AI system performance.

Domain 4: How Should AI Be Used?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I can identify ethical issues that might arise from using AI.
□ I understand what "algorithmic bias" means.
□ I can explain why fairness is important in AI design.

Domain 5: How Do People Perceive AI?

Rate your agreement (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

□ I understand why some people are excited about AI while others are concerned.
□ I can explain common misconceptions about AI.
□ I have thought about how AI might affect my future career.

Note: The full validated instrument contains 25 items across the five domains. Scoring is calculated as the mean response across items within each domain, with an overall AI Literacy score computed as the average of the five domain scores. A 0.5 standard deviation increase (approximately 0.5 points on the 5-point scale) represents educationally significant growth.

INSTRUMENT 2: MENTOR SELF-EFFICACY SURVEY

Purpose

This instrument assesses alumni mentors' confidence in their ability to effectively teach STEM concepts, provide culturally responsive mentorship, and support student learning in robotics and AI. It will be administered before and after mentors complete the AI & Pedagogy Micro-credential.

Administration Protocol

Pre-Training: Beginning of Micro-credential (Month 1 of each cohort year)
Post-Training: Immediately following Micro-credential completion
Mid-Year Check-In: January (after first semester of mentoring)
End-of-Year: May (after Build Season concludes)
Duration: 10-15 minutes
Format: Online survey via Qualtrics

Survey Items

Rate your confidence in your ability to perform each of the following tasks on a scale from 1 (Not at all confident) to 7 (Extremely confident):

Section A: Technical Content Knowledge

1. Explain artificial intelligence concepts (machine learning, neural networks) to high school students using age-appropriate language.
2. Demonstrate how to implement computer vision algorithms for object detection in a robotics context.
3. Troubleshoot autonomous programming issues in Java or Python.
4. Guide students through the process of training a simple machine learning model.
5. Explain the ethical implications of AI systems to teenagers.

Section B: Pedagogical Skills

6. Break down complex technical problems into manageable steps for student learning.
7. Ask effective questions that prompt students to think critically about their code.
8. Provide constructive feedback that builds student confidence.
9. Differentiate instruction to meet the needs of students with varying skill levels.
10. Create a learning environment where students feel comfortable making mistakes.

Section C: Culturally Responsive Mentorship

11. Build trusting relationships with students who share my cultural background.
12. Serve as a positive role model for Black students interested in STEM careers.
13. Address stereotype threat and imposter syndrome when I observe them in students.
14. Connect technical concepts to real-world problems relevant to students' communities.
15. Communicate high expectations while providing appropriate support.

Section D: Mentorship Logistics

16. Manage virtual code review sessions effectively using online collaboration tools.
17. Coordinate with teachers and coaches to align my support with team goals.
18. Allocate my volunteer time effectively to maximize impact on student learning.
19. Navigate school systems and protocols as an external volunteer.
20. Maintain appropriate boundaries and professionalism in my interactions with students.

Open-Ended Questions:

21. What aspects of mentoring high school robotics students do you feel most prepared for?

22. What aspects of mentoring are you most concerned about or unsure how to approach?

23. What additional training or support would help you be more effective as a mentor?

Note: Success metric is 90% of mentors scoring ≥ 5.0 (on 7-point scale) across all items at post-training assessment, representing "confident" to "extremely confident" self-efficacy.

INSTRUMENT 4: TEACHER PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT EVALUATION

Purpose

This instrument evaluates the quality and impact of the summer professional development institutes for robotics coaches and STEM teachers. It assesses perceived relevance, usefulness of materials, changes in teaching practice, and areas for improvement.

Administration Protocol

Immediate Post-Workshop: Final day of 3-day summer institute
Follow-Up: Three months after institute (November) to assess implementation
End-of-Year: May, after full year of implementation
Duration: 15 minutes (immediate); 20 minutes (follow-up)
Format: Online survey via Qualtrics; paper option available

Immediate Post-Workshop Evaluation

Section A: Overall Workshop Quality

Rate your agreement with the following statements (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree):

6. The workshop materials were high quality and useful.
7. I feel more confident teaching AI concepts after this workshop.
8. I would recommend this workshop to colleagues.

___ Introduction to AI and machine learning concepts
___ Progression from block-based to text-based programming
___ Computer vision applications in robotics
___ AI ethics and responsible innovation
___ Alignment with state CS standards
___ Robotics AI Toolkit resources and lesson plans
